1. Objective & Dataset
- This study analyzes the YRBS dataset to examine how school safety risks relate to grade level and mental health (feelings of sadness/hopelessness). 
- The analysis utilizes a large, representative sample of 13,360 students, with a balanced distribution of approximately 2,300 students per grade.

2. Methodology
- Risk Scoring: A weighted School Safety Score (0–100) was built by integrating frequency indicators of carrying weapons, physical fighting,
intravenous drug use, and campus drug dealing to isolate a top-tier High-Risk subgroup.
- Statistical Testing: A Two-Way ANOVA analyzed the main and interaction effects of grade and mental health on safety scores. 
Tukey's HSD post-hoc test was used for pairwise grade comparisons.
- Data Integrity: Codebook inversion biases were corrected for binary and frequency variables 
to ensure prevalence rates precisely match original survey definitions.

3. Key Findings
- Mental Health Impact: The Two-Way ANOVA shows mental health (Sad_Hopeless) has a highly significant main effect (p = 0.0000).
Students experiencing persistent sadness exhibit a mean risk score of 15.66, nearly double the score of 8.73 for those who do not.
- Grade-Level Risk: Grade level exerts a significant main effect (p = 0.0034). 
Tukey's HSD confirms that 9th-graders (11.37) and 10th-graders (11.33) face significantly higher safety risks than 12th-grade seniors (9.93). 
The interaction effect was not significant (p = 0.4616).

4. Conclusion
- Compared to older grades, there are more high-risk students in the lower grades, and psychologically, half of the students are in the sadness group.
So, besides controlling weapons and drugs, counseling interventions are also needed to achieve the best results.
